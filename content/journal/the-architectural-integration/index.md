## 1) Start with the envelope

Solar glass works best when the home is treated as a calm energy envelope. Begin with the roof geometry, shading profile, and an honest load plan. When you know where the sun is, you can decide where the glass belongs.

The architectural question is not “How many panels fit?” but “Which surfaces can carry energy without interrupting proportion?” That framing leads to layouts that feel intentional.

## 2) Treat glass as a material system

Solar glass isn’t a single product; it’s a material system with edges, coatings, fixings, and service access. 

Early coordination with structure and waterproofing is the difference between an elegant surface and an ongoing maintenance story.

## 3) Design for silence and resilience

The best systems are felt in their absence: no flashing alerts, no noisy mechanical decisions, no visible compromise. Resilience comes from sizing storage for real outage scenarios and choosing conversion hardware that stays quiet under load.

> When solar becomes architectural, performance stops competing with beauty. It becomes part of the home’s composure.

If you treat energy as a design layer, you end up with consistent details: concealed components, predictable service paths, and a home that can operate gracefully through change.
